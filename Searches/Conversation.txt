The AHP method is a structured technique for organizing and analyzing complex decisions, based on mathematics and psychology. To use the AHP method to prioritize the industrial units located in the sour gas refinery, you could follow these steps:
- Define the goal and the alternatives. The goal is to prioritize the industrial units based on their degree of dangerousness. The alternatives are the different units that process sour gas, such as acid gas removal, sulfur recovery, tail gas treatment, and sulfur handling.
- Define the criteria and sub-criteria. The criteria are the factors that affect the degree of dangerousness of the units, such as the ones I mentioned before. The sub-criteria are the specific aspects of each criterion, such as the physical and chemical properties, the toxicity and flammability limits, the compatibility and stability, the storage and handling requirements, and the disposal and recycling options of the chemical substances used in the units.
- Construct a hierarchy of the goal, the alternatives, the criteria, and the sub-criteria. The hierarchy is a graphical representation of the decision problem, where the goal is at the top, the alternatives are at the bottom, and the criteria and sub-criteria are in between, forming different levels. Each level contains elements that are compared with respect to the element above them in the hierarchy.
- Assign numerical values to the elements of the hierarchy. This is done by comparing the elements in pairs, using a scale from 1 to 9, where 1 means equal importance or preference, and 9 means extreme importance or preference. The comparisons are based on the judgments of the decision makers, who can use data, experience, or intuition to evaluate the elements. The numerical values are then arranged in a matrix form, where each row and column corresponds to an element, and each cell contains the value of the comparison between the row element and the column element.
- Calculate the priority vectors and the consistency ratios of the matrices. The priority vector is a column vector that contains the normalized weights of the elements in each level of the hierarchy. It is obtained by finding the principal eigenvector of the matrix, which is the eigenvector corresponding to the largest eigenvalue. The consistency ratio is a measure of how consistent the judgments are in the matrix. It is obtained by dividing the consistency index, which is the difference between the largest eigenvalue and the matrix size, by the random index, which is the average consistency index of randomly generated matrices of the same size. A consistency ratio of less than 0.1 is considered acceptable.
- Aggregate the priority vectors and rank the alternatives. The final priority vector of the alternatives is obtained by multiplying the priority vectors of the criteria and sub-criteria by the priority vector of the element above them in the hierarchy, and then summing up the products across the levels. The final priority vector represents the relative importance or preference of the alternatives with respect to the goal. The alternative with the highest priority value is the most preferred one, and the alternative with the lowest priority value is the least preferred one.
These are the general steps of the AHP method. However, there may be variations or modifications depending on the software or tool that you use to implement the method. Therefore, you should always check the instructions and specifications of the software or tool before applying the method. üåç

It involves defining the alternatives, the criteria, and the weights, and then constructing an outranking relation that compares each pair of alternatives based on their performance on the criteria. The alternatives that are not outranked by any other alternative are the best ones.

Sure, I can give you a brief overview of the history, advantages, and disadvantages of each method. üòä
- Pugh Matrix: This method was developed by Stuart Pugh in the 1980s as a technique for comparing and evaluating multiple design options based on a set of criteria. It involves using a reference design and ranking the other designs as better, worse, or same based on each criterion. The method can be extended to use weighted criteria and sensitivity analysis. Some advantages of this method are:
    - It is simple and easy to use, and the outcomes are easy to comprehend.
    - It can deal with both tangible and intangible factors, and compare them using a consistent scale.
    - It can promote discussion and consensus among decision makers and capture different points of view.
Some disadvantages of this method are:
    - The list of criteria is arbitrary and may not be complete or relevant.
    - The scoring methods tend to equalize all the requirements, but some may be more important or essential than others.
    - The values assigned to each option are subjective and not based on quantitative measurements.
- LINMAP: This method was developed by Srinivasan and Shocker in 1973 as a linear programming technique for multidimensional analysis of preference. It involves defining the alternatives, the criteria, and the preference function, and then solving a linear programming problem that minimizes the deviation from the ideal solution. The method can handle interdependent and conflicting criteria. Some advantages of this method are:
    - It is computationally efficient and intuitive for visualizing and exploring multidimensional parameter spaces.
    - It can handle both cardinal and ordinal preferences, and incorporate different types of preference functions.
    - It can check the consistency and robustness of the results and perform sensitivity analysis.
Some disadvantages of this method are:
    - It requires a lot of data and information to define the preference function and the constraints.
    - It relies on the assumption of linearity and additivity of the preferences, which may not hold in reality.
    - It may produce multiple optimal solutions or no feasible solution, depending on the problem formulation.
- TOPSIS: This method was developed by Hwang and Yoon in 1981 as a technique for order of preference by similarity to ideal solution. It involves defining the alternatives, the criteria, and the weights, and then calculating the distance of each alternative from the positive ideal solution and the negative ideal solution. The alternative with the shortest distance from the positive ideal solution and the longest distance from the negative ideal solution is the best one. The method can be applied to various types of data and decision problems. Some advantages of this method are:
    - It is simple and easy to understand and implement.
    - It can rank the alternatives in a complete order and provide a closeness coefficient for each alternative.
    - It can handle both quantitative and qualitative criteria, and incorporate different types of normalization methods.
Some disadvantages of this method are:
    - It requires the positive and negative ideal solutions to be well-defined and non-dominated.
    - It relies on the assumption of independence and commensurability of the criteria, which may not hold in reality.
    - It may produce inconsistent or illogical results, such as rank reversal, circularity, or intransitivity, due to the violation of some mathematical or logical properties.
- ELECTRE: This method is a family of methods for elimination and choice translating reality, which originated in Europe in the mid-1960s by Bernard Roy and his colleagues at SEMA consultancy company. It involves defining the alternatives, the criteria, and the weights, and then constructing an outranking relation that compares each pair of alternatives based on their performance on the criteria. The alternatives that are not outranked by any other alternative are the best ones. The method can deal with three main problems: choosing, ranking, and sorting. Some advantages of this method are:
    - It can handle complex and realistic decision problems with multiple and conflicting criteria.
    - It can incorporate different types of preference functions, thresholds, and veto rules to model the perception of the decision maker.
    - It can provide a partial or complete ranking of the alternatives, or assign them to predefined categories.
Some disadvantages of this method are:
    - It is complex and difficult to understand and implement.
    - It requires a lot of data and information to define the preference functions, the thresholds, and the veto rules.
    - It may produce incomplete or inconsistent results, depending on the problem formulation and the parameters.

Here is a 5 page explanation of the method for conducting an AHP study:
The Analytical Hierarchy Process (AHP) is a structured technique for organizing and analyzing complex decisions based on mathematics and psychology. It was developed by Thomas L. Saaty in the 1970s and has been extensively studied and refined since then. The AHP provides a comprehensive and rational framework for structuring a decision problem, representing and quantifying its elements, relating those elements to overall goals, and evaluating alternative solutions.
There are five major steps in conducting an AHP study:
- Model the problem as a hierarchy containing the decision goal, the alternatives for reaching it, and the criteria for evaluating the alternatives.

## Bing
Analytical Hierarchy Process (AHP) is a structured technique for analyzing complex decisions based on mathematics and psychology. It helps break down a problem into a hierarchy, assess the importance of criteria, compare alternatives and synthesize judgments. Here is a brief overview of AHP:
- AHP was developed by Thomas L. Saaty in the 1970s  as a way to deal with the subjective and qualitative aspects of decision making, as well as the objective and quantitative ones.
- AHP is based on the principle of decomposition, which means dividing a complex problem into simpler and more manageable sub-problems, and the principle of synthesis, which means combining the results of the sub-problems to obtain the final solution.
- AHP uses a hierarchical structure to represent the problem, where the top level is the goal or the objective of the decision, the intermediate levels are the criteria or the factors that affect the decision, and the bottom level is the alternatives or the options that are available for the decision.
- AHP uses pairwise comparisons to elicit the preferences and judgments of the decision makers or the experts on the relative importance of the criteria and the relative performance of the alternatives. Pairwise comparisons are done using a scale of 1 to 9, where 1 means equal importance or preference, and 9 means extreme importance or preference.
- AHP uses eigenvector method to calculate the weights or the priorities of the criteria and the alternatives based on the pairwise comparisons. The weights reflect the relative contribution of each criterion or alternative to the goal of the decision.
- AHP uses consistency ratio to measure the consistency or the rationality of the pairwise comparisons. The consistency ratio compares the actual consistency index of the pairwise comparisons with the expected consistency index of a random matrix. A consistency ratio of 0.1 or less is considered acceptable, while a higher value indicates inconsistency or irrationality in the judgments.
- AHP uses aggregation to synthesize the weights of the criteria and the alternatives to obtain the overall score or the rank of each alternative. The overall score is calculated by multiplying the weight of each criterion by the weight of each alternative under that criterion, and then summing up the products for each alternative. The alternative with the highest overall score is the best one according to AHP.
AHP is a powerful and flexible technique that can handle both qualitative and quantitative aspects of decision making, as well as multiple criteria and multiple alternatives. It can also incorporate the opinions and preferences of different stakeholders or experts, and provide a transparent and rational way to justify and explain the decision. AHP has been widely applied in various fields and domains, such as project management, portfolio selection, resource allocation, risk assessment, strategic planning, and many more  .
